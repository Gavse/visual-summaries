The Core Concept

The Mapping Problem

Why having AI access isn't enough — and what the real bottleneck is.

"The binding constraint is not access to the technology — it is the managerial challenge of discovering where it creates value."
🎯
Uneven Capabilities
AI capabilities are unpredictable — even experts misjudge what it can do.
+

The same model can draft a routine email or redesign an entire product workflow. AI capabilities are uneven across task types and change rapidly. Managers who benchmark AI on one task may dramatically underestimate or overestimate it on another. There is no map.

🔍
Bounded Search
Managers search locally, defaulting to obvious apps while missing high-value uses.
+

The default deployments — chatbot for support, LLM for email — are surface-level. The high-value reorganizations require reimagining the production process itself: which roles disappear, which tasks merge, how the sequence changes. That search space is vast, and managers explore only a tiny portion without guidance.

🔗
Compounding Complementarities
Partial automation preserves bottlenecks. The constraint moves, not disappears.
+

Automating one step in a chain doesn't solve the chain — it just moves the bottleneck to the next human-in-the-loop. Value capture requires reorganizing the entire workflow. This interdependency means the return to AI adoption is non-linear and requires a systems view most firms don't apply.

Research Design

The Experiment

A 3-month field experiment in the INSEAD AI Founder Sprint — the largest randomized trial of AI adoption in startups to date.

515 early-stage startups participated in the INSEAD AI Founder Sprint accelerator program across four global regions. All received identical access: same AI tools, API credits, mentors, and workshops.

The difference was one intervention: the treatment group received weekly case studies showing how AI-native firms had reorganized their production processes — not just that AI helped, but exactly how the workflow changed step-by-step.

The control group received the standard entrepreneurship curriculum — rich in content, but no concrete production reorganization examples.

Stratified randomization ensured balance across regions, industry, and traction stage. Both groups were tracked for business outcomes over the full 3-month program and beyond.

Key design insight: By holding constant all access factors, the experiment isolates the pure effect of discovery — seeing what reorganization looks like changes what founders believe is possible.

Heterogeneity Analysis

The Mapping Problem Doesn't Discriminate

Effects are broad-based: technical background doesn't matter, traction level doesn't matter. The discovery bottleneck is universal.

No differential effect by:
✓
Technical background — Founders with engineering degrees benefit equally to those without. The bottleneck isn't technical skill, it's seeing what reorganization looks like.
✓
Baseline traction — Pre-revenue startups and startups with early customers both benefit equally. The discovery gap is not a function of stage.
✓
Industry sector — Effects broad-based across B2B, B2C, services, and deep tech. AI production reorganization is not sector-specific.
✓
Region — Consistent results across MEA, APAC, Europe, and Americas. The mapping problem is global.

Policy & Practice

What This Means

Three implications that change how we think about AI adoption, education, and the frontier of what's buildable.

🏛️
Access Isn't Enough

API credits, compute subsidies, technical training — these are necessary but not sufficient. Both groups in the experiment had identical access. The control group realized far less value. Policy that focuses only on access is solving the wrong constraint.

🔭
Teach the Discovery Process

Showing HOW other firms reorganized — not just that they did — unlocks search. Case studies and production frameworks expand the visible space for managers. The treatment was not a technical intervention. It was an imagination intervention.

🚀
AI Expands the Frontier

Not just lowering entry barriers. The upper tail grows. AI is changing what can be built, not just how cheaply it can be built. Effects concentrated in top performers suggest AI amplifies capability, not just reduces cost — the best founders get dramatically better, not just marginally.
